Zero Sum and Non-Zero Sum Games

Most games can be categorized as either Zero-Sum Games, where for one player to win, the other one has to lose, or non-zero sum games in which multiple outcomes are possible.

Prisoner's Dilemma

The game is called Prisoner's Dilemma. Each player has to make a decision without knowing what the other player will do. How much both players are winning or losing is determined not by what one player does, but by the combination of their moves.

Game Theory

Almost every game has some form of prisoner's dilemma. When players colaborate with one another, the payoffs for all are greater than when they fight each other.

Geopolitics

The Cold War is an example of prisoner's dilemma. Countries opted to colaborate (i.e. not use nuclear weapons).

Theory of Evolution

Scientists found that prisoner's dilemma can explain why altruism and social cooperation developed for some species.

Business

Competing businesses setting the price of their products is an example of prisoner's dilemma: if only one company lowers the price, it will gain more market share and gain more profits. But if the competition lowers the price as well, both companies are worse off.

Strategies

In the iterative version of the game both players play against each other for multiple rounds. Strategies emerge when players decide to use the knowledge of past moves to try to determine what is the best course of action for the next move.

Always cooperate, or "dove".

This strategy does extremely well if it encounters another player who also chooses to cooperate. When meeting more unscrupulous players, this strategy performs rather poorly. If the other player decides to betray, that other player will get all the benefits.

  • A good guy surrounded by other good guys prospers.
  • If one or more villains join a group of good guys, they will profit and exploit their good character all the way until all good guys go extinct.

Always betray, or "Hawk" strategy

This strategy does extremely well if it encounters players who choose to cooperate. When meeting other "hawk" players, this strategy performs rather poorly. If the other player decides to betray, both players get the penalty.

Retaliate after first strike or "tit-for-tat" strategy

This very simple strategy proved to be the most effective strategy in the long run, when compared to other strategies. Basically, this strategy simulates a good guy with a back bone: "I'll play nice; but if you do me wrong, I'll retaliate".

  • If the other player is always cooperating, or using a tit-for-that strategy, the results will be the same as for a game between 2 "doves", maximizing gains for both players.
  • If it meets a "hawk" player, because it always replicates the opponent's previous move, the strategy acts also as a "hawk", forcing a losing game for both players.
